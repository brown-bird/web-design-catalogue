About

The International Typographic Style — colloquially called Swiss Style — emerged from the design schools of Switzerland in the 1950s. It codified a set of principles that remain the backbone of modern graphic design: grid-based layouts, sans-serif typography, objective photography over illustration, and visual hierarchy achieved through size and weight rather than decoration.

Key figures include Josef Müller-Brockmann, whose grid systems became foundational texts, and Adrian Frutiger, who designed typefaces (Univers, Frutiger) built for clarity at any scale.

Applied to the web, Swiss style manifests as rigorous column grids, precise typographic scale, limited color palettes anchored by a single strong accent (typically red), and a preference for whitespace as an active compositional element rather than empty space.

It is the ancestor of Material Design, most enterprise UIs, and virtually every "clean" modern website. Understanding it is understanding the grammar of contemporary web design.
