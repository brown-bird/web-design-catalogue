Skeuomorphism

Real objects. Digital world.

The design philosophy that bridges the physical and digital by making interfaces look and behave like the real-world objects they replace.

About This Style

Skeuomorphism was the defining aesthetic of the smartphone's first decade. Apple's iOS 6 and earlier macOS designs were canonical examples: the Notes app looked like a yellow legal pad, the calendar had a leather binding, the bookshelf app displayed real wooden shelves with book spines.

The philosophy was rooted in cognitive psychology: users new to touchscreen interfaces would find digital objects more intuitive if they resembled their physical counterparts. The skeuomorphic object taught you how to use it by looking familiar.

Technically, skeuomorphism relies on CSS gradients layered to simulate material properties (glossy plastic, brushed metal, worn leather), multiple box-shadows to suggest depth and light direction, and inset shadows for recessed elements like buttons that appear pressed.

It fell from fashion with iOS 7's flat design rebrand in 2013, when Jony Ive stripped it away entirely. Today it exists in nostalgia, crafted UIs for creative tools, and anywhere tactile delight matters more than minimalist efficiency.
