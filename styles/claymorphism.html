About This Style

Claymorphism emerged in the early 2020s as a playful reaction to the cold minimalism of flat design. Where flat design stripped interfaces down to their barest geometry, claymorphism exaggerates — inflating surfaces, softening every edge, and stacking multiple shadows to create objects that appear to have physical mass.

The technique is distinct from Glassmorphism (which is transparent and frosted) and Neomorphism (which is recessed into the surface). Clay elements sit proud, like lumps of coloured dough pressed onto a background. Each object has a clearly defined front face, depth layers on the sides, and an inset highlight on the top edge that catches imaginary light.

The CSS technique centres on layered box-shadows: a soft outer shadow for ambient light, a harder offset shadow at 4–6px for the "clay depth" layer, and an inset rgba(255,255,255,0.6–0.9) glow at the top to simulate a rounded, light-catching surface. Combined with border-radius values of 24–32px on everything, even rectangular shapes feel organic.

Colour-wise, claymorphism skews pastel: pinks, lavenders, mints, and yellows against off-white or very pale gradients. The result is high-energy without aggression — cheerful, tactile, and immediately approachable. It became widespread in mobile UI design, 3D illustration, and onboarding flows.
