German functionalist school, 1919–1933. Primary colours only. Geometric forms only. No ornament whatsoever. Typography as architecture.

ABOUT THIS STYLE

The Bauhaus school was founded in Weimar, Germany in 1919 by architect Walter Gropius. Its radical proposition: unite fine art and functional craft. The product wasn't painting or sculpture — it was objects, buildings, typefaces, posters. Everything designed for production, nothing designed for decoration alone.

The visual language is instantly recognisable: absolute primary colours (red, blue, yellow, black, white — never mixed), pure geometric forms (circle, square, triangle — never organic curves), and a typographic rigour that treats letterforms as architecture. The grid is sacred. Symmetry serves function. Negative space is active, not empty.

Applied to the web, Bauhaus design means stripping everything to its purpose. Navigation becomes a grid of rectangles. Buttons are actual rectangles — no border-radius, no gradient. Headings are set in a bold geometric sans at scale. Colours are chosen from the four-colour palette and never blended. Decoration is replaced by the deliberate arrangement of functional elements.

The Bauhaus contrast with Swiss International style is instructive: both are rigorous and grid-based, but Bauhaus is more raw — bold primary colour fills where Swiss uses white space, heavy geometric forms where Swiss uses fine type. Both reject ornament, but Bauhaus replaces it with shape; Swiss replaces it with precision. The Bauhaus preceded and directly influenced the Swiss school. It is the root.
