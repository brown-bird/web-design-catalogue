Minimalism

The radical act of restraint. Design defined not by what is added, but by what is withheld.

About This Style

Minimalism in design is not merely the absence of elements — it is the discipline of removing everything until only what is essential remains. Its roots are in Dieter Rams' ten principles of good design and the Bauhaus tradition: that form should follow function, and that a beautiful object is one that fulfills its purpose completely.

Applied to the web, it manifests as vast whitespace, restrained typography, a near-monochromatic palette, and an absence of decoration that would exist anywhere else. The page has space to breathe, and attention falls naturally on what matters.

The key tension in minimalism is between restraint and accessibility. A page can be stripped so bare that it feels cold or fails to communicate hierarchy. The discipline lies in knowing which elements to keep — and ensuring those elements are perfectly chosen.

Minimalist web design often pairs refined serif typography with generous leading and tracking, as the text must carry the full communicative weight that imagery and decoration otherwise share. Great minimalism is not easy — it is the hardest form to execute well.
