VAPOR
WAVE

Retrofuturist digital dreamscape. Hot pink and cyan on deep purple, glitch distortion, and an ironic nostalgia for a future that never arrived.

// ABOUT THIS STYLE

Vaporwave emerged in the early 2010s as an internet microgenre — first in music, then rapidly as a visual aesthetic. It recycles the iconography of 1980s and early 1990s commercial culture: Japanese text, Roman busts, early Windows desktop environments, VHS tracking errors, and the pastel gradients of corporate memphis. All of it rendered through a deep purple-black filter, drowning in neon.

The colour palette is non-negotiable: hot pink (#FF2D7C), electric cyan (#00F5FF), deep purple (#1A0035), and occasionally gold and lavender. These aren't chosen — they're inherited from the CRT phosphor glow of the machines that defined the era. On a dark ground, neon pops with halation and bloom that feels genuinely luminous.

Typography leans hard into VT323, Courier, pixel fonts — anything that evokes a terminal or a low-resolution screen. Text effects matter: the scanline overlay, the subtle glow, occasionally a glitch offset (the cyan channel slides left while the pink channel stays right). These aren't decorations; they're the medium.

The most iconic structural element is the perspective grid — an infinite floor plane receding to a horizon, striped in pink. Above it, a horizontally-banded sunset circle (the "synthwave sun") sits mid-frame. Below the grid, the floor becomes a stage for 3D rendered objects: Greek busts, palm trees, dolphins, Ferraris. All of it bathed in the warm light of a nostalgic parallel dimension.
