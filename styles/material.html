About This Style

Material Design was introduced by Google at I/O 2014 as a unified visual language for Android, web, and cross-platform applications. It draws from the physical world — paper, ink, and the laws of physics — to create a coherent system of surfaces that respond to touch and occupy real space.

The central metaphor is a stack of physical sheets. Each interface element exists at a specific elevation above the base surface, and that elevation determines its shadow, its relationship to other elements, and how it moves. A card at dp2 casts a soft diffuse shadow; a navigation drawer at dp16 sits assertively above all content.

The system is rigorously grid-based: an 8dp baseline grid governs all spacing, making layouts inherently consistent. Typography follows a type scale — Display through Caption — all in Roboto, optimised for screen rendering. Colour follows a primary/secondary palette system where each swatch is defined from 50 (near white) to 900 (near black).

Motion is a core principle, not an afterthought. Transitions follow arcs, acceleration easing, and shared-axis patterns. The Floating Action Button (FAB) represents the single most important action on a screen — always round, always elevated, always pink (or your secondary colour). Material proved that systematic design could scale across thousands of apps without feeling bland.
